# Inventory rules

## Atomic claims

Split compound statements when their parts have different evidence.

Bad:

```text
We are the fastest scheduling platform and save every team ten hours a week.
```

Better:

- Claim A: the product is a scheduling platform.
- Claim B: it is the fastest platform.
- Claim C: every team saves ten hours weekly.

Each claim may have a different status.

## Source quality

Prefer, in order:

1. product behavior or primary internal record;
2. approved first-party documentation;
3. attributable research or customer statement;
4. current public page;
5. inference from multiple sources;
6. unverified note.

A source URL alone does not prove freshness. Record the relevant date or
version when available.

## Evidence labels

### Verified

Use only when a source directly supports the claim. Include an exact file,
section, record, or URL reference.

### Inferred

Use only when the conclusion follows from supplied evidence. State the
inference and its rationale. Do not rewrite it as a verified marketing claim.

### Placeholder

Use for internal layout or workflow testing only. Mark it visibly and include a
replacement owner or requirement.

### Prohibited

Use for:

- known false statements;
- fabricated social proof;
- expired prices or capabilities;
- private or unapproved customer information;
- speculative roadmap represented as current functionality;
- claims the user explicitly forbids.

## Contradictions

Do not silently select the most convenient source. Record both statements and
identify the authority needed to resolve them.

## Task orientation

Organize publishable content around the user's task, not the producing team's
org chart. A section exists because it helps a user understand, decide, or act.

## Safe writing

A safe claim can still be concise. Provenance does not require displaying a
citation beside every sentence, but the working inventory must preserve where
the claim came from.
